# Refund Policy

> Internal knowledge base note: This is a synthetic, airline-neutral customer support reference for a RAG chatbot. It is written in original wording and should be adapted to the operating airline's filed tariffs, fare rules, local laws, and live airport notices before production use.

## Purpose

This document explains refund eligibility, processing, payment handling, exceptions, ancillary refunds, and customer communication standards.

## How Customer Support Should Use This Document

- Use this article to answer general policy questions before checking booking-specific fare rules.
- When a customer's itinerary, ticket condition, immigration status, or disruption record changes the answer, verify the live reservation before giving a final response.
- Explain policy outcomes in plain language and avoid promising exceptions that require airport, revenue management, or regulatory approval.
- For safety, security, legal, and government-entry topics, direct the customer to official authorities when the final decision is outside airline control.

## Refund Policy Summary

Refund eligibility depends on ticket status, fare rules, payment method, reason for refund, country of sale, and whether the airline cancelled or significantly changed the flight.

| Refund Scenario | Typical Outcome | Support Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Voluntary refund | Fare rules apply; fees may be deducted | Check fare brand and unused coupon value |
| Airline cancellation | Refund or rebooking options may be available | Confirm disruption record |
| Duplicate payment | Refund duplicate charge after validation | Collect payment references |
| Tax refund | Unused refundable taxes may be returned | Identify non-refundable carrier charges |
| Ancillary refund | Depends on service use and reason | Check if seat, bag, or meal was delivered |

## Refundable and Non-Refundable Tickets

A refundable ticket allows the customer to receive the eligible fare value back to the original form of payment, subject to conditions. A non-refundable ticket may have no cash refund value after fees, although unused taxes or travel credit may be available.

- Refundable does not always mean fee-free; some fares include service charges or no-show penalties.
- Non-refundable fares may still qualify for refund when required by law or when the airline cancels the service.
- Partially used tickets are recalculated based on flown sectors and the fare construction.
- Promotional fares may have stricter rules than standard economy fares.
- Group, corporate, student, and agency tickets may require special handling.

## Original Form of Payment

Refunds are generally returned to the original payment method to reduce fraud and comply with payment network rules.

- Credit card refunds are returned to the card used for purchase, even if the card has expired or been replaced.
- Bank transfer refunds require account details that match the passenger or payer where local rules require it.
- Cash purchases may require refund through an airline office, travel agent, or bank transfer.
- Wallet, voucher, and gift-card payments are usually refunded back to the same stored-value method.
- Tickets purchased through travel agents should normally be refunded by the issuing agency.

## Refund Timing

Processing time varies by payment method, market, bank, and whether manual review is needed.

- Card refunds may appear after the airline processes the refund and the customer's bank posts the credit.
- Bank refunds can take longer when additional verification or international transfers are involved.
- Refund requests with missing documents, chargebacks, fraud checks, or agency involvement may be delayed.
- Customers should be given a realistic processing range rather than a guaranteed exact date unless system status confirms it.
- Refund confirmation numbers help support teams trace the request.

## Involuntary Refunds

An involuntary refund may apply when the airline cancels a flight, fails to operate as scheduled, causes a missed connection on the same ticket, or makes a significant schedule change.

- Customers should be offered available rebooking options before refund if they still want to travel.
- The refund basis may be full or partial depending on whether any travel was completed.
- Ancillaries linked to the disrupted flight should be reviewed for refund or transfer.
- Refund rights can be affected by local consumer protection rules.
- Support should document the disruption reason clearly in the case notes.

## Refunds for Ancillary Services

Ancillary services include seats, extra baggage, meals, lounge access, priority boarding, Wi-Fi, and pet fees.

- If the service was not provided due to airline action, refund or replacement is usually appropriate.
- If the customer voluntarily changes flights, ancillary transfer depends on availability and service rules.
- Seat fees may be refunded if the airline moves the customer to a lower-value seat for operational reasons.
- Extra baggage may be non-refundable after travel starts unless the bag was not accepted due to airline fault.
- Special meals are usually not refunded separately unless sold as a paid ancillary and not supplied.

## Medical, Bereavement, and Exceptional Refunds

Some airlines allow discretionary refunds or waivers for serious illness, death, visa refusal, military orders, or other exceptional cases. These are not automatic and normally require documentation.

- Medical requests may require a dated certificate stating the customer cannot travel.
- Bereavement requests may require death certificate, relationship proof, and ticket details.
- Visa refusal requests may require an official refusal letter and may still follow fare rules.
- Discretionary waivers should be recorded with the approving department and reason code.
- Sensitive documents must be handled according to privacy rules and retained only as permitted.

## Chargebacks and Duplicate Refund Requests

Customers sometimes contact the airline while also disputing the charge with their bank. Duplicate recovery can delay settlement.

- If a chargeback is active, the refund team may need to wait for payment network resolution.
- Customers should not be promised both a bank dispute credit and an airline refund.
- Duplicate tickets or duplicate payments should be verified through transaction IDs, ticket numbers, and passenger names.
- Refund fraud checks may require additional identity or payment proof.
- Case notes should distinguish a refund request from a payment dispute.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my ticket is refundable?

Check the fare conditions shown during purchase or in the booking record. Support should verify the ticket number and fare basis before giving a final answer.

### Can I get a refund if the airline cancelled my flight?

In many cases, yes. The customer may also choose rebooking when suitable options exist. Local law may provide additional rights.

### Why was my refund less than the ticket price?

The deduction may be a cancellation fee, no-show fee, used-sector value, non-refundable charge, exchange difference, or payment-related adjustment.

### Can my refund go to a different card?

Usually no. Refunds normally return to the original form of payment unless the airline's finance process approves an exception.

### Are seat and baggage fees refundable?

They may be refundable if the airline did not provide the service. Voluntary changes and customer cancellations depend on the ancillary rules.
