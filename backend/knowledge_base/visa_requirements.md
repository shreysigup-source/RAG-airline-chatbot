# Visa Requirements and Travel Documents

## Purpose

This document explains passports, visas, transit documents, electronic authorizations, family documents, and denied boarding due to documentation issues.

## How Customer Support Should Use This Document

- Use this article to answer general policy questions before checking booking-specific fare rules.
- When a customer's itinerary, ticket condition, immigration status, or disruption record changes the answer, verify the live reservation before giving a final response.
- Explain policy outcomes in plain language and avoid promising exceptions that require airport, revenue management, or regulatory approval.
- For safety, security, legal, and government-entry topics, direct the customer to official authorities when the final decision is outside airline control.

## Visa and Travel Document Summary

Customers are responsible for having valid travel documents for every country in the itinerary, including transit points. Airlines perform checks because governments may fine carriers for transporting inadmissible passengers.

| Document | Purpose | Important Note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Passport | Primary international identity document | May need validity beyond travel date |
| Visa | Permission to seek entry or transit | Rules depend on nationality and destination |
| Residence permit | Proof of right to live in a country | May support return or transit eligibility |
| Return or onward ticket | Proof of planned exit | Required by some authorities |
| Health or customs form | Government-required declaration | Often must be completed before departure |

## Customer Responsibility

The airline can provide general guidance, but the customer must confirm requirements with official government sources, embassies, consulates, or authorized visa systems.

- Requirements depend on nationality, passport type, residence status, destination, transit point, purpose of travel, and length of stay.
- A visa exemption for tourism may not apply to work, study, seafarer, crew, business, or long-stay travel.
- Dual nationals should check which passport must be used for exit and entry.
- Children and infants often need their own passports or identity documents.
- Border officers make the final entry decision even when the airline permits boarding.

## Passport Validity and Condition

Many countries require passports to be valid for a minimum period after arrival or departure. Damaged passports can be refused even if technically unexpired.

- Customers should check for torn pages, water damage, peeling laminate, unreadable machine-readable zone, or altered details.
- Some destinations require blank visa pages.
- Emergency passports, temporary passports, and refugee travel documents may have different rules.
- Name changes after marriage or legal update should be reflected consistently across ticket and documents.
- Passports reported lost or stolen may be invalid even if later found.

## Transit Visa Requirements

Transit requirements can apply even when a passenger remains airside and does not pass immigration.

- Some airports require transit visas based on nationality, destination, ticket type, terminal change, or overnight connection.
- Separate tickets may require entry to collect and re-check baggage, triggering normal entry visa rules.
- Airport changes within the same city usually require permission to enter the country.
- Long layovers may exceed visa-free transit limits.
- Customers should check every connection point, not only the final destination.

## Electronic Travel Authorizations and Online Permits

Some visa-exempt travelers still need an electronic travel authorization, pre-travel registration, or digital arrival card.

- Approval can be instant or take several days, so customers should apply early.
- The name, passport number, date of birth, and nationality must match the passport used for travel.
- A new passport normally requires a new authorization.
- Payment confirmation is not the same as approval.
- Airlines may be unable to board a customer when the authorization cannot be verified.

## Minors and Family Travel Documents

Children may need additional documents, especially when travelling alone, with one parent, with relatives, or under guardianship.

- Consent letters may be required by border authorities or recommended for smooth travel.
- Birth certificates or guardianship documents may be requested to prove relationship.
- Unaccompanied minor service rules are separate from government document rules.
- Some countries have strict child-abduction prevention requirements.
- Parents should carry copies of relevant documents and contact details.

## Denied Boarding for Document Issues

Airlines may deny boarding when required documents are missing, invalid, inconsistent, or unverifiable.

- Support should explain that document compliance is required by government authorities.
- Fare refund or change options depend on ticket rules and whether a waiver applies.
- Customers should be advised to contact the embassy, consulate, or official immigration portal.
- Airport agents should record the document reason accurately without giving legal advice.
- A denied boarding due to customer document failure is normally different from airline overbooking or operational denial.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can the airline tell me exactly which visa I need?

The airline can offer general guidance, but customers should verify requirements with official government, embassy, consulate, or authorized visa sources.

### Do I need a visa if I only transit?

Possibly. Transit visa rules depend on nationality, airport, ticket type, terminal transfer, baggage collection, and connection time.

### Can I travel if my passport expires soon?

Maybe not. Many countries require extra validity beyond the travel date, so customers should check destination and transit rules.

### What happens if I am denied boarding for missing documents?

The airline may not be able to carry you. Ticket changes or refunds depend on fare rules and any applicable waiver.

### Does a visa guarantee entry?

No. A visa or authorization allows travel to request entry; border authorities make the final decision.
