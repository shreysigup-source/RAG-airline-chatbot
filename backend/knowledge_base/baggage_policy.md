# Baggage Policy

## Purpose

This document provides detailed guidance for cabin baggage, checked baggage, special items, restricted goods, baggage disruptions, and baggage-related customer service decisions.

## How Customer Support Should Use This Document

- Use this article to answer general policy questions before checking booking-specific fare rules.
- When a customer's itinerary, ticket condition, immigration status, or disruption record changes the answer, verify the live reservation before giving a final response.
- Explain policy outcomes in plain language and avoid promising exceptions that require airport, revenue management, or regulatory approval.
- For safety, security, legal, and government-entry topics, direct the customer to official authorities when the final decision is outside airline control.

## Baggage Policy Summary

Airline baggage rules depend on route, cabin, fare family, frequent flyer status, aircraft limitations, and local security regulations. Customers should check the allowance printed on the ticket or booking confirmation because the same airline may apply different limits on domestic, regional, and long-haul journeys.

| Baggage Type | Common Meaning | Typical Customer Questions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cabin baggage | Carry-on or hand baggage placed in the overhead bin | How many bags can I bring on board? |
| Personal item | Small item placed under the seat in front | Can I carry a laptop bag or handbag? |
| Checked baggage | Bags accepted at the counter or bag drop for loading in the hold | What is my checked baggage allowance? |
| Special baggage | Sports equipment, instruments, mobility aids, or unusual items | Do I need approval for my equipment? |
| Excess baggage | Baggage above the included allowance | How much will extra weight or an extra bag cost? |

## Cabin Baggage, Carry-on Baggage, and Hand Baggage

Cabin baggage is carried into the aircraft cabin by the customer. It must fit safely in the overhead bin or under the seat, must comply with security screening rules, and must not block aisles, exits, or emergency equipment.

- Most economy fares include one cabin bag and one small personal item, but low-cost or basic fares may be more restrictive.
- Premium cabins may allow additional or heavier cabin baggage when aircraft storage permits.
- Cabin baggage must be light enough for the customer to lift into the overhead bin without crew assistance.
- Items exceeding the cabin limit may be checked at the gate and may attract a gate baggage charge.
- On small aircraft, roller bags that meet normal cabin dimensions may still be tagged for delivery at arrival due to limited overhead space.

| Item | Recommended Handling |
| --- | --- |
| Laptop bag | Usually treated as a personal item if it fits under the seat. |
| Duty-free bag | Accepted only when sealed as required by airport security and permitted by the destination or transit airport. |
| Camera bag | Accepted within the cabin allowance unless it exceeds size or weight limits. |
| Coat or umbrella | Normally allowed in addition to the main allowance when space permits. |
| Infant care bag | Accepted when travelling with an infant, subject to screening and space. |

## Checked Baggage Allowance

Checked baggage is transported in the aircraft hold after acceptance by the airline. The allowance may be based on the piece concept, the weight concept, or a combination of both.

- Under the piece concept, the ticket allows a specific number of bags, each with its own maximum weight and size.
- Under the weight concept, the ticket allows a total checked weight that may be divided across bags, subject to per-bag safety limits.
- No single checked bag should exceed the maximum manual-handling weight permitted by the airline or airport.
- Bags must be securely closed, suitable for air transport, and labelled with the customer's name and contact details.
- Customers should remove old baggage tags to reduce the chance of routing errors.

## Domestic, Regional, and International Travel

Baggage allowance can change when an itinerary crosses borders, connects to a partner airline, or includes domestic sectors before or after an international flight.

- Domestic-only tickets often have lower checked baggage allowances than long-haul international tickets.
- International itineraries may follow the baggage rule of the most significant carrier or another rule required by local regulation.
- Separate tickets are normally treated separately, so baggage may not be through-checked unless the airlines and airport process allow it.
- Customers with stopovers may need to collect and re-check baggage depending on customs rules and airport design.
- If a journey includes multiple airlines, customers should review the operating carrier's cabin baggage rules as well as the ticketing carrier's checked baggage rules.

## Infant, Child, Student, and Family Baggage

Family baggage rules vary by fare and destination. Support agents should check whether the infant has a seat, whether a child fare was purchased, and whether student benefits were added before ticketing.

- Infants without a seat may receive a limited checked allowance plus a collapsible stroller, car seat, or carrycot where permitted.
- Children with their own seat generally receive the same allowance as an adult on the same fare type.
- Strollers may be accepted at check-in or at the gate, depending on airport procedure.
- Student baggage offers are usually promotional and may require age, university, visa, or destination proof.
- Family pooling may be allowed under weight concept but is usually limited by maximum weight per individual bag.

## Sports Equipment and Musical Instruments

Special items can often be carried as part of the checked allowance if they meet size, weight, packing, and safety conditions. Some items require advance notice because aircraft hold space is limited.

- Bicycles should be packed in a bike box or protective case with pedals removed or turned inward and handlebars fixed sideways where required.
- Golf bags are usually accepted as one checked item when within weight limits.
- Surfboards, skis, snowboards, and fishing equipment may be subject to length limits and oversize fees.
- Musical instruments may travel as cabin baggage, checked baggage, or on a paid extra seat, depending on size and value.
- Customers should not pack pressurized fuel, flammable waxes, or prohibited repair kits with sports gear.

## Medical Equipment, Mobility Aids, and Assistive Devices

Customers with disabilities or medical needs may carry approved mobility aids and assistive devices in addition to standard baggage allowances, subject to safety and aircraft-loading requirements.

- Wheelchairs and mobility aids should be declared in advance, especially battery-powered devices.
- Lithium battery mobility aids require battery rating, secure installation, and terminal protection checks.
- Portable oxygen concentrators may require airline approval and sufficient battery duration for the journey.
- CPAP machines and medical devices should be carried in the cabin when medically necessary and security rules permit.
- Medication should remain in cabin baggage with prescriptions or supporting documents when possible.

## Lithium Batteries, Power Banks, and Electronic Devices

Lithium batteries are regulated because they can overheat or catch fire if damaged, short-circuited, or packed incorrectly.

- Spare lithium batteries and power banks should travel in cabin baggage, not checked baggage.
- Battery terminals must be protected from short circuit by original packaging, tape, or individual protective pouches.
- Power banks are treated as spare batteries and should not be used or charged when the crew instructs otherwise.
- Installed batteries in laptops, phones, tablets, and cameras are generally permitted when the device is protected from accidental activation.
- Damaged, recalled, swollen, or overheating batteries should not be accepted for travel.

## Liquids, Aerosols, Gels, Powders, and Duty-Free Items

Cabin liquids, aerosols, and gels are controlled by security authorities. The airline may explain the general rule, but airport security makes the final screening decision.

- Many airports apply a 100 ml container limit for liquids, aerosols, gels, creams, and pastes in cabin baggage.
- Containers usually need to fit inside a transparent resealable plastic bag unless the airport has approved newer screening technology.
- Baby food, medication, and special dietary liquids may be allowed in reasonable quantities after screening.
- Duty-free liquids should remain sealed in a security tamper-evident bag with the receipt visible.
- Transit passengers should check the rules of every connecting airport, not only the departure airport.

## Restricted, Fragile, Valuable, and Dangerous Items

Customers should not pack valuables, important documents, medication, keys, electronics, fragile items, or irreplaceable items in checked baggage. Dangerous goods are subject to international safety rules and local law.

- Explosives, fireworks, flammable fuels, corrosives, toxic substances, and many compressed gases are prohibited.
- Sharp objects may be refused in cabin baggage but accepted in checked baggage if securely packed and legal at destination.
- Electronic cigarettes and vaping devices should be carried in the cabin and must not be used on board.
- Smart bags must have removable batteries unless the battery is installed in a way permitted by the airline.
- Fragile items should be packed in hard-sided protective cases; limited-release tags do not remove all customer responsibility.

## Excess, Oversized, and Overweight Baggage

Baggage above the included allowance may be accepted for a fee when space and safety conditions permit. Charges can depend on route, cabin, airport, season, and whether the customer pays online or at the airport.

- Prepaid extra baggage is usually cheaper than airport payment.
- Overweight and oversize charges may apply separately from extra-piece charges.
- Very heavy or very large items may need cargo service rather than passenger baggage service.
- Airport agents may refuse excess baggage when aircraft payload, weather, or operational restrictions apply.
- Refunds for unused prepaid baggage depend on fare rules and local consumer law.

## Lost, Delayed, and Damaged Baggage

Customers should report baggage irregularities before leaving the airport whenever possible. The baggage service desk creates a report that is used for tracing, delivery, and claims handling.

- Delayed baggage reports should include bag tag numbers, customer contact details, delivery address, and a clear bag description.
- Customers should keep boarding passes, baggage receipts, purchase receipts, and repair estimates for claims.
- Essential-item reimbursement is usually limited to reasonable purchases during the delay and may differ by route.
- Damage reports should be made promptly because late claims can be rejected under applicable rules.
- Normal wear, scratches, dents, overpacking damage, and manufacturer defects may be excluded from compensation.

## Packing Recommendations

Good packing reduces delays, damage, and security referrals.

- Place name, phone number, and email inside and outside each checked bag.
- Use sturdy luggage with functional wheels, locks, handles, and zippers.
- Do not overpack, as strained zippers and seams increase damage risk.
- Keep medication, travel documents, electronics, keys, and valuables in cabin baggage.
- Photograph checked bags before travel to help identify them if delayed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I bring both a cabin bag and a backpack?

Usually yes when the backpack fits the personal item size and the fare includes a cabin bag. Basic fares may include only one small item.

### What should I do if my checked bag is delayed?

Report it at the airport baggage desk before leaving, keep the report reference, and update the delivery address if your plans change.

### Can I pack a power bank in checked baggage?

No. Power banks are treated as spare lithium batteries and should be carried in cabin baggage with terminals protected.

### Will the airline pay for fragile items damaged in checked baggage?

Fragile and valuable items should not be checked. Liability may be limited or excluded when damage results from inadequate packing or normal handling.

### Can sports equipment count as checked baggage?

Often yes, if it is packed correctly and remains within the applicable weight and size limits. Oversize or special handling fees may still apply.
